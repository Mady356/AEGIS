# Pediatric Fever — Red Flag Criteria

Any pediatric patient with fever AND one or more of the following
requires referral to a higher level of care or rapid escalation:

- **Inability to drink or breastfeed** — suggests sepsis, severe
  dehydration, or central nervous system involvement
- **Convulsions** — current or in the preceding 24 hours
- **Persistent vomiting** — every fluid intake immediately returned
- **Lethargy or reduced consciousness** — unable to be roused, or
  rouses only with painful stimuli
- **Severe respiratory distress** — chest indrawing, grunting, head
  bobbing, central cyanosis
- **Stiff neck or non-blanching rash** — suggests meningitis or
  meningococcal disease; treat empirically while arranging transfer
- **Capillary refill > 4 seconds** with altered mental status —
  suggests sepsis or severe dehydration

In all of the above, begin empirical management for the most likely
serious condition (broad-spectrum antibiotics if sepsis suspected,
fluid resuscitation, antipyretics) while transfer is arranged.

Document the red flag(s) identified and the time of identification.
The presence of any red flag escalates the triage category to urgent
or immediate, regardless of the underlying febrile illness.
